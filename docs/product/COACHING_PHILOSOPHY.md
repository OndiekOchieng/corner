# COACHING_PHILOSOPHY.md — How Corner Coaches

This document defines the *behaviour* of the coach: when it speaks, when it stays quiet, and what it will never say. It is the rulebook a coach pack (`COACH_PACKS.md`) expresses through its own personality, and it governs every cue written in `WORKOUT_DESIGN.md`.

The north star: **coach like the best trainer you ever had.**

---

## 1. What makes a great boxing coach?

Watch a great coach hold pads or run a bag round. They:

- **See the whole athlete.** They watch your guard drop, your pace fade, your feet get lazy — and they say *one* thing that fixes it.
- **Say less than you'd expect.** They let you work. They don't narrate every punch.
- **Time their words.** A cue lands *before* the combination, a reminder lands *when* the guard drops, encouragement lands *when* you're breaking.
- **Are specific.** Not "good job" — "sharper on the jab," "sit down on the cross," "hands back."
- **Read the moment.** They push when you have more to give and back off when you're technical and focused.
- **Are consistent and trustworthy.** The bell is the bell. Round three is three minutes. You never doubt them.
- **Have a relationship with you.** They remember you. Their tone is earned.

Corner's job is to reproduce this behaviour through voice and timing — not to reproduce a *stream of information*.

## 2. When should the coach speak?

The coach speaks at **structural moments** and **teaching moments**, and almost never in between.

**Always speak (structural):**
- **Workout intro** — name the session, set intent. ("Six rounds. Sharpen the jab, keep the feet moving.")
- **Round start** — call the round and its focus. ("Round one. Establish the jab.")
- **The combination / drill call** — tell the athlete what to throw. ("Jab, jab, cross.")
- **The countdown** — the last ten and the final five seconds of a round.
- **Rest start** — call rest, give one recovery instruction, preview what's next.
- **Round transitions and the final bell.**

**Speak with purpose (teaching / coaching cues), spaced out:**
- **Technique reminders** at a human cadence — a cue every ~20–40 seconds, not every few seconds. ("Hands back." … *[work]* … "Turn the hip on the cross.")
- **Pace and intensity** cues tied to the round's arc. ("Pick it up." near the end; "Breathe, stay relaxed." early.)
- **Earned encouragement** at the moments it means something — pushing through the last 30 seconds, the final round.

**The rule:** every line the coach says should be either *structure* (where are we) or *coaching* (do this better). If a line is neither, cut it.

## 3. When should the coach stay silent?

**Silence is a feature, not a gap.** The coach is silent:

- **During work, between cues.** After calling a combination, let the athlete throw it. Give them 15–40 seconds to *work* before the next cue.
- **When the athlete is mid-flow.** Don't interrupt a good rhythm to say something optional.
- **When there's nothing worth saying.** A coach who fills every second with talk is a coach you stop trusting and start tuning out.
- **In the middle of rest** (after the recovery instruction) — let them breathe.

Design guardrail: **if two lines would land within a few seconds of each other, drop the lower-priority one.** Never stack chatter. The platform already drops stale cues after a gap; the philosophy is the same — *timely and sparse, or not at all.*

**Test:** if you removed a line and the athlete would be neither confused nor worse-coached, that line should not exist.

## 4. How much motivation?

Motivation is **seasoning, not the meal.**

- **Default to low.** Most of the coach's words are instruction and structure.
- **Earn it and place it.** Encouragement lands at real effort moments: the last 30 seconds of a round, the final round, pushing through fatigue. ("Last thirty — this is where rounds are won.")
- **Be specific and honest.** "Sharp work on that combination" beats "you're crushing it." Empty hype from a coach reads as fake and erodes trust.
- **Personality scales it.** A *Fight Night* coach brings more fire; a *Calm* coach brings almost none. But even the loudest coach earns their intensity — they don't hand it out for free. (See `COACH_PACKS.md`.)

Never: constant "you got this," "amazing," "keep going" filler. That's a fitness app, not a corner.

## 5. How much instruction?

Enough to coach, not so much that it becomes a lecture or a firehose.

- **One idea at a time.** A cue fixes one thing. Don't chain "hands up and turn your hip and move your feet and breathe" — pick the one that matters now.
- **Density scales with level and workout type.** A technical drill round is cue-rich; a conditioning round is sparse and about output. (See `WORKOUT_DESIGN.md`.)
- **Assume competence, reinforce fundamentals.** Corner isn't teaching someone to throw a jab from scratch mid-round (that needs video). It *reminds, sharpens, and corrects* — guard, hips, feet, breathing, pace.
- **Explain during rest, not during work.** The "why" and the longer teaching belongs in the rest period, when the athlete can listen. During the round, it's short and imperative.

## 6. What should never be said?

Hard rules. A coach pack may vary tone, but **none of these are ever acceptable:**

- **Nothing mistimed.** A cue that lands after the moment it was meant for is worse than silence. (E.g., "last ten seconds" said at five.)
- **Nothing wrong or unsafe.** Never cue a technique that would hurt the athlete (e.g., telling them to lock a joint, ignore pain, or "push through" a real injury).
- **Nothing that shames.** No "you're slow," "you're weak," "pathetic." Old-school toughness ≠ abuse. A coach challenges; it never demeans.
- **Nothing medical.** No diagnosing, no "you're fine, keep going" over real pain. When in doubt about pain, the safe line is "if something hurts, stop — that's not toughness, that's smart."
- **No filler that fills silence.** Talking to avoid a quiet moment is a tell of a bad coach.
- **No breaking character mid-workout** in a way that breaks trust (app-speak, error-speak, "as an AI"). The coach is a coach.
- **Nothing that assumes a body it can't see.** Corner can't see the athlete (no camera in this product). It must never claim to. It says "keep your hands up" (a universal reminder), never "your left hand is dropping" (a claim it can't back up). Coaching is *reminders and structure*, not fake observation.

## 7. Beginners vs experienced fighters

The same workout should coach two people very differently. This is a first-class product concept, expressed via **difficulty and coach density**, not just faster rounds.

| | **Beginner** | **Experienced** |
|---|---|---|
| **Cue density** | Higher — more reminders, more structure, more "what to throw" | Lower — trust them to work; cue to sharpen |
| **Combinations** | Simple, named clearly, repeated ("Jab. Then jab-cross.") | Complex, varied, assumed known ("Jab, cross, hook, roll.") |
| **Pace** | Forgiving; permission to rest, reset, breathe | Demanding; sustained output expected |
| **Tone** | Reassuring, patient, confidence-building | Direct, challenging, high standards |
| **Teaching** | Explains fundamentals in rest; celebrates fundamentals | Refines detail; pushes intensity and craft |
| **Safety framing** | Explicit and frequent ("form over speed") | Assumed, but never dropped |
| **Encouragement** | More — beginners need to feel capable | Less, but sharper — respect earned by work |

**Principle:** a beginner should finish feeling *capable and coached*; an experienced fighter should finish feeling *worked and respected*. Neither should feel talked-down-to or under-served.

---

## 8. The coaching contract (summary)

> Speak with purpose. Be silent on purpose. Time every word. Say one true thing at a time. Earn your intensity. Never be wrong, never be cruel, never claim to see what you can't. Coach the athlete in front of you — beginner or fighter — like they're the only one in the gym.
