# VOICE_GUIDELINES.md — How Corner Sounds

`COACHING_PHILOSOPHY.md` defines *when* the coach speaks and *what* it may say. This document defines *how it sounds* when it does. These rules apply to every line of copy and every coach pack; personalities bend tone within them, never break them.

The voice is heard over exertion, breathing, and bag impact — often from a phone on the floor. Everything here serves **being understood in one pass, at the right instant, without the athlete looking.**

---

## 1. Tone

- **Present and grounded.** The coach is *in the room with you*, not narrating from a booth. Second person, direct. "Hands back." not "The athlete should keep their hands up."
- **Confident, never uncertain.** No "maybe," "try to," "if you can." A coach commits. "Sit down on the cross." not "Maybe try to put more weight into it."
- **Warm authority.** It cares that you get better, and it holds a standard. Warmth without standards is a cheerleader; standards without warmth is a drill sergeant. Corner is a coach.
- **Human, not robotic.** Contractions, natural phrasing, real coach language. "Let's go" not "Commence exercise."

## 2. Cadence & timing

- **Cue *before* the action, not during.** Combination calls land a beat *ahead* so the athlete can execute on the word. "Jab, cross —" then they throw.
- **One breath per line.** If the coach can't say it in a single relaxed breath, it's too long for mid-round.
- **Leave working room.** After a cue, silence for 15–40 seconds (see philosophy §3). The rhythm is *call → work → call*, not *call → call → call*.
- **Never overlap.** Two lines never collide. If timing forces a choice, the higher-priority line wins and the other is dropped, not queued into a pile-up.
- **Countdown is sacred and exact.** "Ten seconds." then, at the end, "Five. Four. Three. Two. One." — bell. This is the most trust-critical timing in the app; it must be perfect.

## 3. Sentence length

| Context | Length | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Mid-round cue** | 1–4 words | "Hands back." · "Double the jab." |
| **Combination call** | 2–6 words | "Jab, cross, hook." |
| **Countdown** | 1–2 words | "Ten seconds." · "Three." |
| **Round intro** | one short sentence | "Round three. Work the body." |
| **Rest instruction** | 1–2 short sentences | "Rest. Breathe deep, shake it out." |
| **Rest teaching** | 1–3 sentences (this is the *only* place longer talk belongs) | "Next round we add the hook. Remember to turn the hip — the power comes from the ground, not the arm." |

**Rule:** the harder the athlete is working, the shorter the line. Longer thoughts wait for rest.

## 4. Vocabulary

- **Speak boxing.** Jab, cross, hook, uppercut, slip, roll, guard, stance, angle, output, combination, the body, the head. Real terms, used correctly.
- **Concrete verbs.** "Snap the jab," "sit down on the cross," "turn the hip," "cut the angle," "pop off the bag." Not "do more," "go harder" in the abstract.
- **Plain and universal.** Avoid gym slang that only some regions use; avoid jargon a returning boxer wouldn't know. When a term might be unfamiliar (a beginner workout), name-and-explain it once in rest, then just use it.
- **No numbers-as-punches unless taught.** Corner defaults to named punches ("jab, cross") over the numbering system ("one, two") because numbers aren't universal — unless a coach pack explicitly teaches and uses them.
- **Say it the way it's read aloud.** Copy is written for the ear, not the eye. Punctuation exists to control pacing and breath, not grammar. Uppercase names are spoken as words, not spelled out.

## 5. Urgency & energy

Energy is **dynamic** — it tracks the arc of the round and the workout, it isn't a flat setting.

- **Round opening:** calm, deliberate. Setting up. ("Round one. Find your range.")
- **Round mid:** steady, working. Cues are instructional, even-toned.
- **Round final 30s:** energy rises. This is the push. ("Last thirty. Empty the tank.")
- **Countdown:** peak urgency, but *controlled* — clipped and clear, not shouted into mush.
- **Rest:** energy drops all the way down. Recovery voice. Slow, low, calm.
- **Final round / final bell:** the biggest the coach gets — earned, and then release.

Energy is also a **coach-pack dial**: a *Calm* coach's "peak" is another coach's baseline. But every pack still *moves* — a monotone coach is a boring coach.

## 6. Silence

- Silence is the default state during work. The coach is not "waiting to talk"; it's *watching you work* (in spirit) and choosing not to interrupt.
- A minimum spacing between non-structural cues keeps the soundscape from feeling anxious or naggy.
- After the last rest instruction, the rest period is mostly silent — the athlete recovers.
- If nothing is worth saying, say nothing. Confidence to be quiet is part of the voice.

## 7. Examples — good coaching

> **Round intro:** "Round two. Body and head. Start doubling the jab."
> *[works ~25s]*
> **Cue:** "Turn the hip on the cross."
> *[works ~30s]*
> **Cue:** "Hands back after you punch."
> *[works]*
> **Pace (final 30s):** "Last thirty — pick up the output."
> **Countdown:** "Ten seconds." … "Five. Four. Three. Two. One." — *bell*
> **Rest:** "Rest. Breathe deep, drop the shoulders. Next round we add the hook off the jab."

Why it's good: sparse, timed, one idea per cue, boxing-specific, energy that rises into the countdown, teaching saved for rest.

## 8. Examples — poor coaching (and why)

> ✗ "Okay so now you're going to want to try to maybe throw some punches, like a jab or whatever feels good, and keep it up, you're doing amazing!"
> **Why bad:** hedging, vague, no boxing content, empty hype, way too long for mid-round.

> ✗ *[non-stop, every 2 seconds]* "Jab. Cross. Good. Nice. Hook. Keep going. Yes. Punch. Move."
> **Why bad:** firehose, no working room, no teaching, becomes noise the athlete tunes out.

> ✗ "Great job, you're crushing it, you're a beast, unstoppable!" *[repeated every round]*
> **Why bad:** unearned, generic hype; a cheerleader, not a coach; erodes trust.

> ✗ *[at 5 seconds left]* "Okay, ten seconds to go!"
> **Why bad:** mistimed. The single most trust-destroying error. Better to say nothing than to be wrong about time.

> ✗ "Your right hand is dropping, fix your left foot angle."
> **Why bad:** claims to *see* the athlete (Corner can't) and stacks two corrections. Use a universal reminder, one at a time: "Hands back."

---

## 9. Voice checklist (for every line)

1. Could the athlete understand it in one pass, mid-effort, without looking? 
2. Is it one idea, in one breath? 
3. Is it boxing-specific and concrete? 
4. Does it land *before/at* the right moment, never after? 
5. Would the coach be *right* — never claiming to see, never mistimed, never unsafe? 
6. If I deleted it, would the athlete be worse off? (If no, delete it.)

## 10. Amendment — PR-020: the opening line & time of day

Two rules for the **session opening**, so the first thing the athlete hears is right
(design in [`../coaching-runtime/SESSION_INTRODUCTIONS.md`](../coaching-runtime/SESSION_INTRODUCTIONS.md)):

- **Never hard-code the time of day.** "Tonight", "this morning" and the like break
  immersion the moment they're wrong. Default to **timeless wording**. A coach may
  reference time only as a rare, personality-specific flourish, selected from injected
  `timeOfDay` data with a neutral fallback — so it can never be spoken at the wrong hour.
  (The engine passes time in; the coach never reads a clock.)
- **The opening voices the workout, not a template.** State the session's objective/focus
  in the coach's own words ("Today's focus: {focus}."), then hand off to round one — one
  idea per breath, same checklist above. The workout supplies the facts; the Coach Pack
  supplies the voice. Which coach references time, and how warmly they greet, is part of
  their personality (`PERSONALITY_SYSTEM.md`).
