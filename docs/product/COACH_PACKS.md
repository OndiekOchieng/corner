# COACH_PACKS.md — The Coaches

A coach pack is a **personality**, not a skin. It is a distinct trainer with a teaching style, an energy, a vocabulary, and a philosophy — expressing the *same* workout structure and the *same* coaching rules (`COACHING_PHILOSOPHY.md`, `VOICE_GUIDELINES.md`) through its own character.

The same round — same combinations, same timing — should feel like a **different session** depending on who's in your corner. That is the point. People choose the coach they connect with, and that choice is a core part of why Corner is Corner and not a timer.

---

## 1. What a coach pack IS and ISN'T

**A coach pack IS:**
- A consistent *character* — voice, phrasing, energy, and coaching values.
- A *dial-setting* on the shared rules: cue density, motivation level, tone, energy range.
- A relationship the athlete builds over time.

**A coach pack IS NOT:**
- A different set of workouts (workouts are shared; the coach *voices* them).
- A license to break the coaching rules (never mistimed, never wrong, never cruel, never claims to see the athlete — these bind *every* pack).
- A voice-actor swap with identical scripts. Two packs coaching the same round say genuinely different things.

**Design invariant:** a pack expresses the workout's structural events (round start, combination, cue, countdown, rest, finish) in its own voice. It never changes *what happens* — only *how it's coached and how it sounds*.

---

## 2. The pack dials

Every pack is defined by five dials. This is the template for authoring any new coach.

- **Teaching style** — how it corrects and instructs (detailed & technical ↔ instinctive & feel-based).
- **Energy** — its baseline intensity and range (calm & low ↔ loud & driving).
- **Vocabulary** — the words and register it uses (precise boxing terms ↔ old-gym slang ↔ plain & warm).
- **Cue frequency** — how much it talks (cue-rich ↔ sparse, lets you work).
- **Motivation** — how much and what kind of encouragement (almost none ↔ constant fire), always *earned*.

## 3. The launch coaches

Six personalities that span the range of real trainers. Together they cover the athletes in `PRODUCT.md §3` and the workout types in `WORKOUT_DESIGN.md §9`.

### 3.1 The Technical Coach — "Detail wins fights."
- **Teaching style:** Precise, analytical, mechanics-obsessed. Explains the *why* (in rest). Cares about the small things — guard, hip rotation, weight transfer, foot position.
- **Energy:** Moderate, even, focused. Rarely raises the voice; intensity comes from precision, not volume.
- **Vocabulary:** Correct, specific boxing terminology. "Rotate off the ball of the foot." "Retract the jab on the same line."
- **Cue frequency:** High. This is the most talkative coach — but every cue is a correction worth making.
- **Motivation:** Low and technical. Praise is for *clean work*: "That's the shot — same thing again."
- **Best for:** Skill drills, defense & counters, athletes who want to get *better*, not just tired.

### 3.2 The Old School Coach — "Less talk. More work."
- **Teaching style:** Instinctive, no-nonsense, fundamentals-first. Believes in reps and sweat over analysis. Says one thing and lets you figure out the rest.
- **Energy:** Grounded, steady, unbothered. Gravel, not fireworks.
- **Vocabulary:** Gym-floor plain, a little old-fashioned. "Stick that jab." "Sit down on it." "Don't get lazy."
- **Cue frequency:** Sparse. Long stretches of silence while you work. When he talks, you listen.
- **Motivation:** Rare and gruff — which makes it land. "Now *that's* a round." Tough, never cruel.
- **Best for:** Experienced athletes, conditioning, people who find chatter annoying and want to grind.

### 3.3 The Fight Night Coach — "This is the round. Let's go."
- **Teaching style:** Tactical and in-the-moment, like a corner during a bout. Less about mechanics, more about *fighting* — pace, output, finishing.
- **Energy:** High and driving, with a big range — calm setup, explosive finishes. The most dynamic pack.
- **Vocabulary:** Cornerman urgency. "Empty the tank." "This is where rounds are won." "Dig to the body."
- **Cue frequency:** Moderate, bursty — quiet in the setup, loud in the push.
- **Motivation:** High but *earned and placed* — saved for the final 30 seconds, the last round, the moment you're breaking. Never empty hype.
- **Best for:** Fight simulation, peak workouts, athletes who train to feel like a fighter.

### 3.4 The Calm Coach — "Breathe. Stay smooth."
- **Teaching style:** Composed, mindful, flow-oriented. Coaches rhythm, relaxation, and breathing as much as punches. Believes tension is the enemy.
- **Energy:** Low and steady throughout — its "peak" is another coach's baseline. Never rushes, never shouts.
- **Vocabulary:** Warm, simple, calming. "Stay loose." "Long, easy breaths." "Smooth and sharp."
- **Cue frequency:** Low–moderate, unhurried. Comfortable with silence.
- **Motivation:** Quiet reassurance over fire. "Good — that's the rhythm."
- **Best for:** Recovery/technique flow, stress-relief training, beginners who are intimidated, morning sessions.

### 3.5 The Competition Coach — "Champions are built in the boring rounds."
- **Teaching style:** Demanding, high-standard, program-minded. Treats the athlete like a competitor in camp — accountability, consistency, no shortcuts. Cares about doing it *right when it's hard*.
- **Energy:** Intense but controlled — disciplined pressure rather than hype.
- **Vocabulary:** Serious, standards-driven. "Hold the guard when you're tired — that's the round that counts." "Don't cheat the rest, don't cheat the work."
- **Cue frequency:** Moderate, pointed. Cues target discipline and consistency, not just technique.
- **Motivation:** High-standard, not warm-fuzzy. Approval is earned by *not slipping* under fatigue. Challenges the athlete to be better than yesterday.
- **Best for:** Serious/returning athletes building a real program, conditioning, people who want to be pushed.

### 3.6 The Southpaw Coach — "The world's built for orthodox. Use it."
- **Teaching style:** Stance-specialist. Coaches specifically for the southpaw fighter — angles, the lead-hand game, foot positioning against orthodox, the straight left. Deep expertise in the details lefties actually need.
- **Energy:** Confident, sharp, a little contrarian — proud of the southpaw craft.
- **Vocabulary:** Stance-aware and precise. "Step outside the lead foot." "Straight left down the middle." "Own the angle."
- **Cue frequency:** Moderate–high on stance-specific detail.
- **Motivation:** Identity-driven — makes the southpaw athlete feel their stance is an *advantage*, not an afterthought.
- **Best for:** Southpaw athletes (badly served by generic apps), and orthodox fighters drilling against southpaws. A clear differentiation few products offer.

## 4. How one round sounds across packs (illustrative)

The *same* event — a round focused on the jab, entering its final push — voiced by each coach:

| Pack | Final-30s line |
|---|---|
| Technical | "Last thirty. Keep the jab on the same line — don't loop it when you're tired." |
| Old School | "Thirty seconds. Don't get lazy on that jab." |
| Fight Night | "Last thirty — this is the round! Pump that jab, empty it out!" |
| Calm | "Thirty seconds left. Stay smooth, keep the jab long and easy." |
| Competition | "Thirty seconds. This is where you separate — hold your standard." |
| Southpaw | "Last thirty. Straight left, step to the angle, finish sharp." |

Same structure, same timing, six different corners. That is the coach pack promise.

---

## 5. Authoring rules for future packs

1. Fill in all five **dials** and write a one-line **philosophy** (the coach's belief).
2. Coach the **same events**; never invent structure or change timing.
3. Obey every rule in `COACHING_PHILOSOPHY.md` and `VOICE_GUIDELINES.md` — packs bend tone, never break trust.
4. Be **distinct** — if a new pack sounds like an existing one on the same round, it isn't a pack yet.
5. Stay **true to boxing** — a coach pack is a real trainer archetype, not a gimmick voice.
