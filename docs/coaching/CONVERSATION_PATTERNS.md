# CONVERSATION_PATTERNS.md — The Recurring Shapes of Coaching Speech

A coach is not improvising from nothing. Underneath the words are a small number of **recurring patterns** — the moves a coach makes over and over, in every session, in every sport. Naming them gives every Corner author a shared toolkit: pick the pattern the moment calls for, then voice it in the coach's personality.

Vocabulary comes from `../workouts/CUE_LIBRARY.md`; personality voicing from `PERSONALITY_SYSTEM.md`; *when* from `TIMING_MODEL.md` and `ROUND_DIRECTING.md`.

> These are patterns, not scripts. The shape is fixed; the words rotate and the coach's character colors them. A pattern used at the wrong moment is still the wrong coaching.

---

## How to read each pattern

For every pattern: **Purpose · Typical wording · When to use · When NOT to use.** Wordings are examples to rotate, not fixed lines (`CUE_LIBRARY.md §7`).

---

## 1. Instruction

- **Purpose:** tell the athlete what to do — the forward-looking cue that drives the next action.
- **Typical wording:** "Jab and step." · "Sit down on the cross." · "Off the balls of your feet."
- **When to use:** the default coaching move; setting up and refining through opening and development.
- **When NOT to use:** mid-combination; stacked on the last cue; when the athlete is already doing it well (that's a moment for silence, not another instruction).

## 2. Correction

- **Purpose:** fix one mechanic, framed as the *next rep*, never as criticism of the last.
- **Typical wording:** "Tighter on the hook — turn, don't swing." · "Same line — don't loop it."
- **When to use:** the moment a fix is actionable and there's airspace; one thing at a time.
- **When NOT to use:** never chain two; never claim to see the flaw ("your hook is looping" → fake observation); not under peak load when the athlete can't process it; not on the close.

## 3. Reinforcement

- **Purpose:** confirm a good thing so the athlete keeps it — earned, specific praise.
- **Typical wording:** "That's the snap — keep it." · "Now the feet are moving." · "That combination got sharp."
- **When to use:** right after the athlete genuinely does something well; at low points to rebuild; sparingly.
- **When NOT to use:** generically or on a loop ("good, good, good"); when unearned (kills trust — `COACH_PERFORMANCE.md §2`); as a substitute for a needed correction.

## 4. Reminder

- **Purpose:** keep the always-true fundamentals live without nagging — guard, breathing, stance.
- **Typical wording:** "Hands back." · "Chin down." · "Breathe with the shots." (rotate hard — most-repeated category).
- **When to use:** woven through development and pressure, especially when fatigue predicts the lapse.
- **When NOT to use:** identically every time (looping destroys it); so often it becomes wallpaper; over a more important cue.

## 5. Challenge

- **Purpose:** raise the demand — ask for more without shaming.
- **Typical wording:** "One more, sharper." · "Don't cheat the round." · "This is where it counts."
- **When to use:** pressure and closing phases; late rounds; when the athlete has more in the tank.
- **When NOT to use:** as an insult ("don't be lazy" is a challenge; "you're lazy" is shaming — never the second); in recovery rounds; on a beginner who's already at their edge.

## 6. Recovery

- **Purpose:** bring the athlete down and restore them — in rest, or after a hard burst.
- **Typical wording:** "Breathe deep, drop the shoulders." · "Shake it out." · "Slow the breath down."
- **When to use:** rest periods (front-loaded), recovery rounds, after a peak.
- **When NOT to use:** as hype in disguise; filling the whole rest with it; rushing the athlete through recovery.

## 7. Urgency

- **Purpose:** the push — concentrate everything into the final effort.
- **Typical wording:** "Last thirty — empty it out." · "Dig now." · "Everything you've got."
- **When to use:** final ~30s, last round, the session's peak.
- **When NOT to use:** too early (nothing left for the real push); in a recovery/technical round; as constant shouting (it stops meaning anything — `MOTIVATION_MODEL.md`).

## 8. Confidence

- **Purpose:** shift the athlete's self-belief — from doing the skill to owning it.
- **Typical wording:** "This is your round." · "Trust your work." · "Own the outside."
- **When to use:** when competence has been earned; at higher levels; before a hard round; on the close.
- **When NOT to use:** as empty hype; before the skill exists (build competence first — confidence follows, `PROGRESSION_MODEL.md §7`); on repeat.

## 9. Teaching

- **Purpose:** the *why* — the longer explanation that deepens understanding.
- **Typical wording (1–3 sentences):** "That hook lands because the jab set it up. Next round, feel how the jab opens the door." 
- **When to use:** **rest only** — the single window with spare cognition (`ROUND_DIRECTING.md §6`).
- **When NOT to use:** ever mid-round; when the athlete is still gassed and can't listen; at a length that runs into the next round.

## 10. Focus

- **Purpose:** gather scattered attention back to one thing.
- **Typical wording:** "Sharp mind, loose body." · "One thing — the jab." · "Stay composed."
- **When to use:** when the work is drifting; before a demanding round; to reset after a ragged burst.
- **When NOT to use:** as filler; so often it becomes noise; when the athlete is already locked in (leave them alone).

## 11. Sequencing patterns across a round

Patterns combine into a natural coaching conversation over the round's arc:

```
OPENING       Instruction (setup) · Focus
DEVELOPMENT   Instruction → [work] → Correction → [work] → Reinforcement · Reminder
PRESSURE      Challenge · Reminder(guard) · short Instruction
CLOSING       Urgency · Confidence → [clear air] → countdown
REST          Recovery → Teaching → preview(Instruction) → quiet
```

Two rules bind the sequence:
- **Never two patterns in one breath.** Each needs its own moment and its working room.
- **Correction and Reinforcement don't touch.** Separate them in time or they cancel (`COACH_PERFORMANCE.md §3`).

---

## 12. The pattern contract

> A coach draws on ten recurring moves — instruct, correct, reinforce, remind, challenge, recover, urge, build confidence, teach, refocus. Each has a right moment and a wrong one. Choose the pattern the moment calls for, place it where it belongs on the arc, voice it in the coach's character, and give it room — and the athlete hears a coach in conversation with them, not a script on shuffle.
