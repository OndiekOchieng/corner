# TIMING_MODEL.md — When the Coach Speaks (and When It Doesn't)

Timing is what separates a coach from a talking clock. The *same words* land as coaching or as noise depending entirely on **when** they arrive. This document defines when Corner speaks, when it deliberately doesn't, and how that changes across every phase of a session.

Builds on `COACH_PERFORMANCE.md §6` (the decision to speak) and `SILENCE_GUIDE.md`. Cue density baselines come from `../workouts/CUE_LIBRARY.md §2`.

> A cue at the wrong time is worse than no cue. Right word, wrong moment, is a distraction wearing a coach's voice.

---

## 1. The two clocks

The coach tracks two things at once:

- **Structural time** — the fixed, must-be-exact moments: round start, combination calls ahead of the beat, the final-ten countdown, the bell, rest start, finish. These are **non-negotiable and precise** (`§7`). The athlete sets their body by them.
- **Coaching time** — the flexible, *chosen* moments: a technical cue, a reminder, a push, an encouragement. These fit into the gaps *between* structural moments and are governed by judgment, not a schedule.

A timer only has structural time. A coach has both — and spends most of its skill on the second.

---

## 2. When cues happen

A coaching cue earns its moment when:

- **The athlete can act on it now.** A correction lands when the next rep can apply it — not after the mistake has passed into the next thing.
- **There's airspace.** No structural moment is imminent (don't cue over an approaching countdown), and the last cue has had room to be worked on.
- **It fits the arc.** Setup cues early, refinement mid, output/heart late (`§4`, `ROUND_DIRECTING.md`).
- **It's spaced.** Baseline one coaching cue per **20–40 seconds** of work — a rhythm of *speak, then let them work.*

---

## 3. When cues should intentionally not happen

Silence is a positive choice at specific moments (full treatment in `SILENCE_GUIDE.md`):

- **During earned flow.** The athlete is punching in rhythm and it's clean — do not interrupt to say it's clean. Let them own it.
- **Immediately after a cue.** The working room *is* the coaching. Two cues back-to-back means the first never got applied.
- **In the middle of a combination.** Never speak across the punches you just called.
- **Approaching a structural moment.** Clear the air before the countdown, the bell, a combination call.
- **When there's nothing worth saying.** Talking to fill silence is the single most common way a coach becomes a timer.
- **When the athlete is gassed and needs to just work.** In a conditioning grind, less talk, more room to breathe.

---

## 4. Timing across the session

Each phase has its own speech signature. (Round-internal phases are detailed in `ROUND_DIRECTING.md`; this is the whole-session view.)

### Warmup
- **Density:** low, calm, priming.
- **Purpose:** set intent, find range, loosen — announce *what today is about* and let the body wake up.
- **Silence:** generous. This is not the time for dense correction; it's the time to establish tone and trust.

### Early round
- **Density:** measured. Establish the round's focus, then a first mechanical cue or two.
- **Purpose:** point attention at this round's one thing. Set up before you refine.
- **Silence:** deliberate gaps — let the athlete find the movement before you shape it.

### Mid round
- **Density:** the round's working rhythm — steady, instructional, one idea per cue, well spaced.
- **Purpose:** refine mechanics, layer application, keep the guard and breathing honest.
- **Silence:** between every cue. This is the *speak-then-work* heartbeat of the whole app.

### End of round (final ~30s)
- **Density:** rising, shorter, fewer words, more heart.
- **Purpose:** the push — output, composure under fatigue, "this is where rounds are won." Then the coach clears the air for the countdown.
- **Silence:** the beat *right before* the countdown — a held breath so the count lands clean.

### Rest
- **Density:** front-loaded then quiet. One recovery cue, then a short *teaching* window (the only place multi-sentence explanation belongs), then a preview of what's next, then **quiet** to let them recover.
- **Purpose:** recover the body, teach the "why," set up the next round. Never fill the whole rest with talk.
- **Silence:** the back half of rest is mostly quiet — recovery is the job, not listening.

### Workout finish
- **Density:** one honest, specific close. Then stop.
- **Purpose:** land the sense of *having trained* — specific to what was done, in character, never generic (`ROUND_DIRECTING.md §E`, `MOTIVATION_MODEL.md`).
- **Silence:** after the close, complete silence. The session is over; don't diminish it with a coda.

---

## 5. Timing shape, at a glance

```
WARMUP      low ······· calm, intent-setting, lots of room
EARLY       ▁▂  establish focus → first cues
MID         ▂▃▂▃▂▃  speak-then-work heartbeat
END-30      ▃▄▅  rising, shorter → [clear air] → COUNTDOWN (exact)
BELL        ▐ exact
REST        ▅▂▁  recover-cue · teach · preview · quiet
FINISH      ▂  one honest close · then silence
```

Energy and density both **build across the round** and **build across the session** (peak late), then release. A flat density line is the signature of a timer.

---

## 6. Why silence is sometimes the best coaching

Because coaching is about the athlete's *work*, not the coach's voice:

- **Silence lets the rep happen.** A cue is a seed; silence is the soil. No quiet, no application.
- **Silence signals trust.** Going quiet while the athlete flows says "that's right — keep it," louder than praise would.
- **Silence protects attention** for the moments that must land (countdown, bell, the one key correction).
- **Silence lets rhythm live.** Boxing has its own timing; a coach talking over it destroys the thing being trained.
- **Silence prevents fatigue-of-voice.** A coach always talking becomes wallpaper; the athlete stops hearing any of it. Sparing speech keeps every word valuable.

Full doctrine in `SILENCE_GUIDE.md`. The short version: **the coach's silence is not the absence of coaching — it is coaching.**

---

## 7. The precision non-negotiables

Some moments are *exact* or the whole product breaks:

- **The countdown** — "Ten seconds," then "Five. Four. Three. Two. One." aligned to real time. The most trust-critical lines in Corner.
- **The bell** — on the boundary, every time.
- **Combination calls** — a half-beat *ahead* of when the athlete should throw, so they can act on the call.
- **Rest start / round start** — precisely on the transition, so the athlete's body can trust the structure.

These never flex for stylistic reasons. A coach may vary *what* it says everywhere else, but these moments are fixed points the athlete builds their body around — and getting one wrong costs more trust than a hundred good cues earned.

---

## 8. The timing contract

> Structural moments are exact; coaching moments are chosen. Speak when the athlete can act, in the gaps, on the arc, one idea at a time — and go quiet to let the work happen, to protect the moments that must land, and whenever there is nothing worth the athlete's attention. Density rises with the round and the session, then releases. Right word, right moment — or silence.
