# ROUND_DIRECTING.md — Every Round Is a Performance

A round is not a block of time to be filled — it is a **three-minute performance with an arc**, and the coach is its director. The athlete supplies the work; the coach shapes its rise and fall. This document defines the phases *inside* a round and the coach's responsibility in each.

Pairs with `TIMING_MODEL.md` (the whole-session view) and `ROUND_LIBRARY.md` (round archetypes). The energy arc echoes `../product/VOICE_GUIDELINES.md §5`.

> Direct the round like a fight: a quiet start, a building middle, a hard push, a clean finish, and honest recovery. The athlete should *feel* the shape even with their eyes closed.

---

## 1. The five phases of a round

Every working round moves through the same dramatic arc, regardless of archetype. The durations flex; the sequence doesn't.

```
OPENING → DEVELOPMENT → PRESSURE → CLOSING → RECOVERY(rest)
 setup      refine        push       peak       reset & teach
 calm       steady        rising     clipped    low
```

A Footwork round and a Fight Conditioning round have the *same arc* — only the content and intensity differ. Recognizing the arc is what lets one coach direct any round and have it feel like Corner.

---

## 2. Opening (first ~20–30%)

**The moment:** the round has just begun. The athlete is settling in and needs to know *what this round is for.*

**Coach responsibilities:**
- **Name the round and its focus** — clear, short: "Round three. Body work." The athlete orients instantly.
- **Set the tone** for this specific round (calm for technique, driving for conditioning).
- **Establish before refining.** Let the athlete find the movement for a few beats before you shape it. First cue is a *setup*, not a correction.
- **Low density.** A cue or two, lots of room.

**Failure modes:** diving straight into corrections before the athlete has thrown anything; a generic opening that could belong to any round; over-talking the setup.

---

## 3. Development (the working middle, ~30–60%)

**The moment:** the athlete is into the work. This is the round's *teaching-in-motion* heart.

**Coach responsibilities:**
- **Refine, one thing at a time.** Sharpen the mechanic the round is about — spaced, `speak-then-work`.
- **Layer, don't pile.** Introduce → drill → begin to combine, matching the round's archetype and the athlete's level.
- **Keep the honest reminders live** — guard, breathing, stance — rotated in wording so they never loop (`../workouts/CUE_LIBRARY.md §7`).
- **Steady density** — the round's working rhythm; the most cues live here, but still one idea at a time with room between.

**Failure modes:** stacking corrections; nagging the same word identically; introducing brand-new complexity the athlete can't absorb mid-round (that belongs in rest/teaching).

---

## 4. Pressure (the build, ~60–85%)

**The moment:** the round is asking a question — can the athlete hold form as the work gets hard?

**Coach responsibilities:**
- **Raise the intensity** in voice and demand — bursts, output, "step in behind the jab."
- **Guard the form that fatigue attacks first** — the guard dropping, the feet stopping. Coach against the *predictable* decay ("hands up while you press").
- **Shorten the cues.** As physical load rises, cognitive load must fall — fewer words, sharper (`COACH_PERFORMANCE.md §5`).
- **Apply, don't introduce.** This is where the skills from development get *used* under pressure — never the first appearance of a new one.

**Failure modes:** peaking too early (nothing left for the close); introducing new technique under load; long sentences the gassed athlete can't hold.

---

## 5. Closing (final ~30 seconds → countdown → bell)

**The moment:** the round's climax. Where rounds are won.

**Coach responsibilities:**
- **The push** — heart and output, shortest words, most energy the coach will use in this round: "Last thirty — empty it out." "Dig now."
- **Then clear the air.** A held beat before the countdown so it lands alone (`TIMING_MODEL.md §7`).
- **The countdown — exact.** "Ten seconds." … "Five. Four. Three. Two. One." Precise, clipped, the peak of the round's energy.
- **The bell — on the boundary.** Then an immediate drop.

**Failure modes:** a mistimed countdown (fails the whole round and the athlete's trust); talking over the count; a push that's all volume and no instruction; no discernible peak (the round just... ends).

---

## 6. Recovery / rest (the reset and the teaching window)

**The moment:** the bell has gone. The athlete is blowing, dropping the heart rate. This is the *only* place for real teaching.

**Coach responsibilities:**
- **Recover first.** One recovery cue — "Breathe deep, drop the shoulders." Let the body come down before you ask the mind to listen.
- **Then teach** — the single place multi-sentence explanation belongs: *why* the last round mattered, one thing to carry forward. Brief, calm, at most a few sentences.
- **Preview what's next** — "Next round, we add the hook." The athlete steps into the next round already oriented.
- **Then go quiet.** The back half of rest is mostly silence — recovery is the job, not listening (`TIMING_MODEL.md §4`, `SILENCE_GUIDE.md`).

**Failure modes:** filling the whole rest with talk (the athlete can't recover); hyping instead of recovering; teaching so long the athlete is still processing when the next round starts.

---

## 7. Directing the whole session

The rounds are not independent — the *session* has an arc built from them:

- **Warmup** is the overture: intent and tone, low stakes.
- **Early rounds** open calm and technical; **middle rounds** build and layer; **late rounds** peak (pressure, fight-pace, simulation).
- **The hardest round comes late**, not first — the session builds like a single long round.
- **Cooldown is the honest close** — bring it all the way down, reinforce the day's objective, and land the sense of having trained (`MOTIVATION_MODEL.md`, `../workouts/QUALITY_CHECKLIST.md §4`).

A well-directed session and a well-directed round are the same shape at different scales: **open, build, peak, release, close honestly.**

---

## 8. The directing contract

> Treat every round as a performance with five phases — open calm and set the focus, develop by refining one thing at a time, build pressure while protecting the form fatigue attacks, close with heart and an exact countdown, then recover and teach in the rest. Shape the session the same way at the larger scale. The athlete should *feel* the arc rise and fall — that feeling is the difference between being coached and being timed.
