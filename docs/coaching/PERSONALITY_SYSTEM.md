# PERSONALITY_SYSTEM.md — The Coaches as True Personalities

A coach pack is not a voice skin — it is a **person.** The same workout, coached by two different Corner coaches, should feel like training with two different humans who happen to know the same boxing. This document expands the six packs from `../product/COACH_PACKS.md` into full personalities an author can write consistently.

Every coach shares the non-negotiables (`COACH_PERFORMANCE.md`): perfect timing, no fake observation, nothing unsafe or shaming, earned praise, silence as a tool. Personality is the *character* built on that shared foundation — never an excuse to break it.

> Personality is not *what* the coach knows — every coach knows the same boxing. It's *how they teach it, when they push, what they find funny, and how they make you feel.* Change those, keep the boxing correct, and you have a person.

---

## The personality dimensions

Each coach is defined on eleven axes. Fill all eleven and the coach writes itself:

**Teaching philosophy · Temperament · Vocabulary · Humour · Patience · Intensity · Correction style · Rest style · Round introductions · Workout introductions · Workout endings.**

---

## 1. The Technical Coach

*The master craftsman. Boxing is a system to be understood, and every detail matters.*

- **Teaching philosophy:** precision over power; understand *why* before you drill *what*. Mastery is the motivation.
- **Temperament:** measured, focused, quietly exacting. A scientist of the craft.
- **Vocabulary:** precise mechanical language — "rotate the hip," "same line," "recover the guard," "short and straight." Names things accurately.
- **Humour:** dry and rare; an occasional wry note about a common mistake. Never at the athlete's expense.
- **Patience:** very high. Will drill one detail as long as it takes; never rushes understanding.
- **Intensity:** moderate and controlled; intensity serves the technique, never overrides it.
- **Correction style:** frequent but gentle and exact — one detail at a time, framed as the next rep. "Sharper — snap it back on the same line."
- **Rest style:** the teacher's window — explains the *why* clearly and briefly, connects this round to the last.
- **Round introductions:** names the round and its precise objective. "Round two. The cross — we build it from the floor up."
- **Workout introductions:** frames the session as a craft to sharpen. "Today we make the straight right a real weapon. Precision first."
- **Workout endings:** acknowledges the specific technical gain. "The cross found its line today. That's real progress. Well trained."
- **Best for:** technical, defense, and skill-development workouts; any level, especially learners who want to understand.

## 2. The Old School Coach

*The gym veteran. Decades in the game, few words, high standards, deep respect earned slowly.*

- **Teaching philosophy:** fundamentals, discipline, and toughness. You earn it; nothing is given.
- **Temperament:** gruff, grounded, unhurried. Seen everything, impressed by little, loyal once you've earned it.
- **Vocabulary:** old-gym language — "hands up," "sit down on it," "work," "again." Short. No frills.
- **Humour:** deadpan, occasional, earned. A blunt one-liner about doing the work.
- **Patience:** high for effort, low for excuses. Will let you struggle because struggle is the lesson.
- **Intensity:** steady and demanding; expects work without needing to shout for it.
- **Correction style:** blunt, minimal, standard-setting. "No. Hands back. Again." Never cruel, never soft.
- **Rest style:** brief and no-nonsense — one thing to fix, then quiet. Recovery is expected, not fussed over.
- **Round introductions:** plain and direct. "Round three. Body work. Get to it."
- **Workout introductions:** sets the standard, not the mood. "Nothing fancy today. Fundamentals, done right. Let's work."
- **Workout endings:** sparing, weighty praise. A "good work" from this coach is worth a hundred from anyone else. "That was honest work. Good."
- **Best for:** fundamentals, power, and conditioning workouts; athletes who value grit over polish.

---

## 3. The Fight Night Coach

*The corner during the war. Big-moment energy, reads the round like a bout, lives for the finish.*

- **Teaching philosophy:** train for the fight — pace, pressure, composure under fire. Boxing is combat, coached as combat.
- **Temperament:** electric, present, rising — the voice in your corner between rounds of a real fight.
- **Vocabulary:** fight language — "this is the round," "walk him down," "make him miss, make him pay," "dig." Dramatic but true.
- **Humour:** confident and warm; the swagger of someone who's been in real corners. Light, not clownish.
- **Patience:** moderate — patient in setup, insistent when it's time to go.
- **Intensity:** high and *dynamic* — the widest range of any coach, from a low pre-fight calm to a roaring finish. The dynamics are the point.
- **Correction style:** urgent but framed as fight-readiness. "Guard up — he'll make you pay for that." (Note: framed as *your* readiness, never fake observation of a real opponent — `CUE_LIBRARY.md §4`.)
- **Rest style:** the between-rounds corner — quick recovery, one sharp adjustment, and a charge into the next round. "Good round. Next one, we press. Breathe."
- **Round introductions:** raises the stakes. "Round four. This is where fights are won. Let's go."
- **Workout introductions:** sets the fight. "Tonight we train like it's the real thing. Composed early, dangerous late."
- **Workout endings:** the post-fight corner — proud, spent, real. "That's a fighter's session. You dug when it got hard. Respect."
- **Best for:** fight-simulation, pressure, and counter-punching workouts; athletes who want the big-fight feeling.

## 4. The Calm Coach

*The steady presence. Lowers the pressure, breathes with you, makes hard work feel sustainable.*

- **Teaching philosophy:** calm is a skill; a relaxed fighter is a better fighter. Sustainable training over grinding.
- **Temperament:** serene, warm, unhurried. The most silent of the coaches, and the most comfortable in quiet.
- **Vocabulary:** soft and grounding — "smooth," "easy," "loose," "breathe," "flow." Gentle verbs.
- **Humour:** warm and light; a quiet reassurance more than a joke.
- **Patience:** the highest. Never rushed, never anxious about silence or slow progress.
- **Intensity:** low to moderate; even the pushes are calm — "steady, strong." Lowers arousal rather than raising it.
- **Correction style:** the softest — offered, not imposed. "Let the jab come back a little easier." Never sharp.
- **Rest style:** true recovery — breathing, dropping the shoulders, long quiet. The gold standard for restful rest.
- **Round introductions:** gentle and grounding. "Round two. Nice and smooth — find your rhythm."
- **Workout introductions:** sets a calm intent. "Let's move well today. Relaxed, clean, no strain."
- **Workout endings:** warm and settling. "That was good, sustainable work. You stayed loose the whole way. Well done."
- **Best for:** recovery, technique-flow, and beginner workouts; anxious athletes, light days, anyone who needs the pressure taken down.

---

## 5. The Competition Coach

*The performance director. Prepares fighters for real stakes; demanding, precise, treats you like a pro.*

- **Teaching philosophy:** championship standards. Details win fights; the hard rounds are where you're made.
- **Temperament:** intense, exacting, professional. High expectations delivered with belief in the athlete.
- **Vocabulary:** performance language — "championship rounds," "hold the standard," "compose and fire," "don't cheat it." Elevated, serious.
- **Humour:** minimal and focused; this coach is here to work. Warmth shows as belief, not jokes.
- **Patience:** moderate — patient with honest effort toward the standard, impatient with coasting.
- **Intensity:** high and sustained; the most consistently demanding coach. Expects the athlete to meet a real bar.
- **Correction style:** exact and elevated to a standard. "That's not championship level yet — turn the hip fully, every rep."
- **Rest style:** professional cornering — brief recovery, a precise adjustment, and a demand for the next round. "Good. Now the next round is the one that counts. Recover, then give me everything."
- **Round introductions:** frames rounds as tests. "Round five. The championship round. This is what we train for."
- **Workout introductions:** sets a serious bar. "We train to a real standard today. No coasting. Earn every round."
- **Workout endings:** the demanding coach's respect — hard to earn, meaningful when given. "You met the standard today. That's what fighters do. Excellent work."
- **Best for:** conditioning, fight-simulation, and advanced workouts; serious athletes who want to be pushed to a real bar.

## 6. The Southpaw Coach

*The specialist. Coaches the left-hander's actual game and makes the athlete feel their stance is a weapon.*

- **Teaching philosophy:** the southpaw game is its own art, not a mirror of orthodox. The stance is an edge — learn to wield it.
- **Temperament:** knowing, confident, a little conspiratorial — a specialist sharing hard-won secrets most coaches can't teach.
- **Vocabulary:** stance-specific — "straight left on the line," "own the outside foot," "own the angle," "make him turn." Precise to the southpaw game.
- **Humour:** wry, insider — the shared knowingness of being the awkward one in the ring, and glad of it.
- **Patience:** high — this game takes patience to build correctly, and the coach knows it's rarely taught well.
- **Intensity:** moderate and building; intensity in service of owning the angle and the outside position.
- **Correction style:** stance-aware and identity-affirming — corrects toward the southpaw's real advantages. "Step outside his lead foot first — then the left. That's your edge."
- **Rest style:** teaches the southpaw tactics specifically — the foot battle, the angle, why the straight left is the money punch. Affirms the game.
- **Round introductions:** frames the southpaw's weapon. "Round two. The straight left — down the middle, on the line. Your best punch."
- **Workout introductions:** affirms the stance as an advantage. "Today we build the southpaw game — the real one. Your stance is a problem for them. Let's sharpen it."
- **Workout endings:** identity-building and proud. "That's the southpaw edge — use it. Most fighters never learn to deal with it. Great work."
- **Best for:** southpaw-specific and any stance-aware workouts; the athletes every other app fails.

## 7. Writing a coach consistently

- **Pick the coach before you write a line.** The character shapes every cue, not just the intros.
- **Stay in character across all eleven axes** — a coach whose corrections are gentle but whose close is a drill-sergeant bark isn't a person, it's a shuffle.
- **Personality never overrides the non-negotiables.** No coach is ever late on a countdown, claims to see the athlete, shames, or endangers — character lives *within* the trust rules, never outside them.
- **The same boxing, a different human.** When you rewrite a workout for a new coach, the *events and technical content are identical* — only the voice, timing texture, humour, and framing change (`FIRST_COACH_PERFORMANCE_PACK.md` proves this).

---

## 8. The personality contract

> Each Corner coach is a full person defined on eleven axes — philosophy, temperament, vocabulary, humour, patience, intensity, correction, rest, and the three framing moments (round intro, workout intro, workout ending). Every coach teaches the same correct boxing and honors the same trust rules; they differ in *how they make the athlete feel.* Write all eleven consistently and the athlete trains with a human, not a voice pack.
