# PROGRESSION_MODEL.md — How Athletes Improve

A Corner workout meets the athlete where they are and moves them forward. This document defines *forward* — what improvement means at each level, and how authors raise intensity, complexity, and confidence without just "making it harder." It is the backbone of difficulty (`WORKOUT_SCHEMA.md`) and the reason the catalog is a curriculum, not a pile.

Grounded in `../product/COACHING_PHILOSOPHY.md §7` and `../product/WORKOUT_DESIGN.md §5–6`.

> Progression is not "faster and less rest." It's **more skill, more composure, more capacity — coached to a higher standard.**

---

## 1. The three dimensions of progression

Every athlete advances along three axes, at different rates:

1. **Complexity** — from single punches → combinations → combinations with defense → angles and tactics.
2. **Intensity / capacity** — from short, forgiving work → sustained output → fight pace under fatigue.
3. **Confidence / composure** — from "I can do this" → "I can do this when it's hard" → "I own this."

A good progression raises **one axis at a time** in a given workout, and balances all three across a pack. Raising all three at once overwhelms and breaks form (and trust).

---

## 2. How beginners improve

**Where they are:** can throw basic punches; unsure on mechanics, pace, and stamina; need structure and reassurance.

**Improvement looks like:** cleaner single punches, holding a guard, basic footwork, finishing a session, and *believing they belong on the bag.*

**Author for beginners by:**
- **One skill, drilled patiently.** Single jab, then jab-cross — not five-punch combinations.
- **High cue density, reinforcing.** More reminders, more "what to throw," frequent guard/breathing cues.
- **Forgiving structure.** Shorter rounds (2 min), generous rest, explicit permission to reset and breathe.
- **Form over speed, always.** "Sharp, not fast." Celebrate clean fundamentals.
- **Confidence first.** More earned encouragement; they need to *feel capable* to come back.

**Success:** finishes feeling coached and capable, with one skill visibly cleaner. **Never:** overwhelmed, shamed, or gassed and defeated.

---

## 3. How intermediates improve

**Where they are:** solid fundamentals; ready to combine skills, add defense, and sustain more work.

**Improvement looks like:** flowing combinations, integrating defense with offense, holding pace across a full session, and tactical awareness starting to appear.

**Author for intermediates by:**
- **Layering skills.** Combinations, then combinations with a slip or a counter; the spiral (introduce → drill → combine → pressure).
- **Balanced cue density.** Sharp technical cues, but trust them to work between them.
- **Standard timing and rest.** 3-min rounds, 1-min rest.
- **Introduce tactics and pace changes.** Fight-pace and pressure archetypes enter here.
- **Raise the standard.** Expect the guard to hold, the feet to keep moving, form to survive a full round.

**Success:** finishes worked, with a skill *integrated* (not just isolated). This is Corner's core athlete.

---

## 4. How advanced athletes improve

**Where they are:** strong technically and physically; want to be pushed, refined, and treated like a fighter.

**Improvement looks like:** high output with intact form under fatigue, sophisticated defense and counters, ring craft, and composure in the hard rounds.

**Author for advanced athletes by:**
- **High complexity.** Long combinations, defensive layers, angles, feints, counters chained together.
- **Sparse, sharp cues.** Trust them. A single precise correction is worth more than constant talk.
- **Demanding capacity.** Tighter rest, fight-pace and simulation rounds, sustained high output.
- **The hardest standard.** The coaching targets what slips *when tired* — "hold the guard in the last thirty," "don't cheat the round."
- **Ring IQ.** Tactical cues become central: cutting the ring, setting traps, controlling distance.

**Success:** finishes genuinely worked and *respected*, with detail sharpened and capacity extended. **Never:** patronized or under-challenged.

---

## 5. How intensity increases

Raise intensity by turning several dials, in this rough order of preference:

1. **Output** — more punches, more sustained work within a round.
2. **Pace variation** — bursts and flurries (fight-pace) rather than one flat tempo.
3. **Standard under fatigue** — expecting form to hold late.
4. **Round length / rest** — longer work, tighter rest (use *last*, sparingly; never the only lever).

Intensity should *build across the session* (peak late) and *build across a pack* (later workouts demand more).

---

## 6. How complexity increases

The complexity ladder, in order:

```
single punch → two-punch combo → multi-punch combo → combo + defense → combo + defense + angle → tactical sequence (feint/counter/pressure)
```

Introduce each rung only when the previous is solid (`prerequisites` in the schema enforce this). Complexity is added in *development/technical* rounds, then *applied* in pressure/fight-pace rounds — never introduced for the first time at fight pace.

---

## 7. How confidence increases

Confidence is a designed outcome, not an accident:

- **Competence breeds confidence.** Drilling a skill until it's clean is the primary confidence builder.
- **Earned, specific encouragement.** "That combination got sharp" beats generic hype.
- **Progressive challenge.** Each workout is *slightly* beyond the last — stretch, not overwhelm. Repeated failure kills confidence; repeated earned success builds it.
- **Ownership language at higher levels.** "This is your round," "own the outside" — the athlete moves from *doing* the skill to *owning* it.
- **The honest close.** Finishing a session, acknowledged specifically, is a confidence deposit every time.

---

## 8. How southpaw progression differs

Southpaw progression is **not** orthodox progression mirrored — the *tactics* differ, so the curriculum differs.

- **Foundations differ:** the lead-hand game and the straight left are central earlier; the jab-cross relationship is different against orthodox opponents.
- **The foot battle is a first-class skill:** winning the outside-foot position vs. orthodox is a progression thread orthodox fighters never train.
- **Angles are earlier and heavier:** the southpaw's advantage is angular; progression emphasizes stepping to angles sooner.
- **Identity matters:** southpaw progression should make the athlete feel their stance is an *edge*, building confidence in a game built against them (see the Southpaw coach in `../product/COACH_PACKS.md`).

Author southpaw progressions as their own ladder — from owning the outside and the straight left, to angle attacks, to countering the orthodox jab, to full southpaw pressure/fight-pace — not as flipped orthodox workouts.

---

## 9. How conditioning progression differs from technical development

These are **two different ladders** and should not be conflated:

| | **Technical development** | **Conditioning** |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Skill: cleaner, more complex, more tactical | Capacity: more output, more endurance, form-under-fatigue |
| Cue style | Dense, corrective, one-thing-at-a-time | Sparse, output/breathing/pace-driven |
| Progression | Complexity ladder (§6) | Volume, pace variation, tighter rest, longer work |
| Success | The skill is sharper/integrated | The athlete did *more work* and held form |
| Coach energy | Focused, instructional | Driving, breathing-and-pace, encouragement earned late |

A complete athlete progresses on **both** ladders. A good pack alternates and eventually *combines* them (a fight-pace round that demands both capacity and skill) — but an author must be clear which ladder a given round/workout is climbing, and coach it accordingly.

---

## 10. The progression contract

> Move the athlete forward on **one axis at a time**, to a **higher standard**, with **earned confidence** — and make each session *slightly* beyond the last. Beginners leave capable; intermediates leave integrated; advanced athletes leave worked and respected. Southpaws climb their own ladder; conditioning and skill are two ladders, both climbed.
