# AUTHORING_GUIDE.md — How to Write a Corner Workout

This is the starting point for any coach authoring a Corner workout. It teaches *how to think* about building a session so that everything you create feels unmistakably like Corner. The companion documents give you the parts: `WORKOUT_SCHEMA.md` (the fields), `ROUND_LIBRARY.md` (round archetypes), `CUE_LIBRARY.md` (the words), `PROGRESSION_MODEL.md` (how athletes improve), and `QUALITY_CHECKLIST.md` (the gate every workout passes).

Read the product foundation first: `../product/COACHING_PHILOSOPHY.md`, `../product/VOICE_GUIDELINES.md`, and `../product/WORKOUT_DESIGN.md`. This guide assumes them.

> **Author like a coach, not a content producer.** You are writing a training session you'd be proud to run with a real athlete on a real bag — one who can't see you, only hear you.

---

## 1. Before you write a single round

Answer four questions. If you can't, you're not ready to author.

1. **Who is this for?** Beginner, intermediate, advanced? Orthodox, southpaw, both? (See `PROGRESSION_MODEL.md`.)
2. **What is the one objective?** A Corner workout teaches *one main thing* well — "establish the jab," "learn to slip and counter," "build fight-pace conditioning." Not five things poorly.
3. **What's the arc?** How does it warm up, build, peak, and cool down?
4. **Which coach is this written for?** The default voice, and which coach packs suit it. (See `../product/COACH_PACKS.md`.)

A workout without a clear single objective is the most common failure. Fix it here, before rounds.

---

## 2. How a coach creates a workout (the process)

1. **Name it with intent.** The name states the promise: "Orthodox Power," "Slip & Counter," "Fight Conditioning." (Naming rules in `WORKOUT_SCHEMA.md`.)
2. **Write the objective** in one sentence — the thing the athlete leaves better at.
3. **Choose the arc and duration** — number of rounds, round length, rest length, appropriate to the level.
4. **Design the warmup** — prepare the *specific* work to come, not a generic loosen-up.
5. **Build the rounds** — pick archetypes from `ROUND_LIBRARY.md`, order them so each builds on the last, and give each round one focus.
6. **Place the cues** — using `CUE_LIBRARY.md`, at the right density and moments; save teaching for rest.
7. **Design the rest** — recovery + the teaching window + a preview of what's next.
8. **Design the cooldown** — bring it down, reinforce what was learned, close honestly.
9. **Run it in your head, out loud.** Read every line aloud, in real time, imagining the athlete on the bag. This is the single best quality check.
10. **Pass the checklist** (`QUALITY_CHECKLIST.md`) before it ships.

## 3. How workouts are structured

Every Corner workout has the same skeleton (detailed in `../product/WORKOUT_DESIGN.md`):

```
Warmup  →  Round 1 → Rest → Round 2 → Rest → … → Round N  →  Cooldown
           (each round: establish → work → push → countdown → bell)
```

- **Warmup** prepares *this* session's work.
- **Rounds** are the body; each has **one focus** and they **relate** — round 2 builds on round 1; a combination introduced early returns later.
- **Rest** recovers, teaches, and previews.
- **Cooldown** lowers intensity, reinforces the objective, and closes.

**The building block is the round, and the round's building block is the cue.** Get the round focus right, then get the cue placement right.

---

## 4. How sessions flow

A good session has *momentum* — it feels like it's going somewhere.

- **Open deliberately.** The first round establishes; don't open at full intensity.
- **Build across rounds.** Layer complexity or raise output as the session progresses.
- **Peak, then release.** The hardest/most immersive round comes late (a conditioning push, a fight-simulation finish), followed by cooldown.
- **Teach in a spiral.** Introduce a skill, drill it, add to it, then use it under pressure — across the session, and across a pack. The athlete should *feel* they built something.

Avoid: flat sessions where every round is interchangeable, and front-loaded sessions that peak in round one and fade.

---

## 5. How difficulty increases

Difficulty is **multi-dimensional** (see `PROGRESSION_MODEL.md` and `../product/WORKOUT_DESIGN.md §6`). Raise it by turning several dials, not just speed:

- **Complexity** — longer/more varied combinations, added defense, angles.
- **Output** — sustained work, tighter rest, higher pace.
- **Cue sparseness** — advanced athletes get fewer, sharper cues; they're trusted to work.
- **Standard** — expectations under fatigue ("hold the guard when you're tired").

The *same content* (the jab) appears at every level, coached completely differently. Never make a "harder" workout just by shortening rest.

---

## 6. How workouts teach skills

Corner reminds, sharpens, and corrects — it does not teach a punch from scratch mid-round (that needs video Corner doesn't have). So author for an athlete who can already throw, and teach by:

- **Naming the skill and its intent** at the round start.
- **Drilling it** with well-spaced cues that fix *one thing at a time*.
- **Explaining the "why" in rest** — the teaching window, where longer thought belongs.
- **Layering** — add to the skill only once the base is drilled.
- **Using it under pressure** later — apply the skill in a conditioning or fight-pace round so it's not just isolated reps.

Never claim to *see* the athlete (Corner can't). Teach with universal reminders and structure, not fake observation. (`../product/COACHING_PHILOSOPHY.md §6`.)

## 7. Example — a good workout (sketch)

**Name:** Slip & Counter (Intermediate, Orthodox) · **Objective:** learn to slip the jab and counter with the cross.

- **Warmup:** light footwork, range-finding jabs, gentle rolls — priming head movement.
- **R1 — Establish the jab:** find range, sharp single jabs. *(Base before defense.)*
- **R2 — Slip the jab:** introduce the slip, slow and clean; cue timing and staying balanced.
- **R3 — Slip and counter:** slip, then fire the cross. The core skill.
- **R4 — Under pressure:** slip-counter at fight pace, string it into combinations.
- **Cooldown:** shadow the slip-counter slowly, reinforce the mechanic, close.

Why it's good: one objective, a spiral that builds the skill and then pressures it, rests that teach the "why," and a clear arc.

## 8. Example — a poor workout (and why)

**Name:** "Boxing Workout 3" · **Objective:** *(none)* · Rounds: random combinations, no relationship, cues fired constantly.

- ✗ **No objective** — the athlete leaves better at nothing in particular.
- ✗ **Interchangeable rounds** — no build, no spiral, no reason for the order.
- ✗ **Cue firehose** — talking every few seconds; no working room; becomes noise.
- ✗ **Rest as dead air** — just a countdown, no recovery guidance, no teaching, no preview.
- ✗ **Generic name** — promises nothing; feels like a timer preset.

This is exactly what Corner is *not*. If a workout could be any workout, it isn't a Corner workout.

---

## 9. The author's oath

> Every workout has one clear objective, an arc that builds, rounds that relate, cues that are timely and sparse, rest that recovers and teaches, and a close that means something. If I read it aloud in real time and it feels like *coaching*, it's ready. If it feels like a *timer with talking*, it isn't.
