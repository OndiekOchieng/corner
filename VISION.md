# Vision

## The cause

Many people own a heavy bag. Very few people have a coach.

A bag hangs in a lot of garages and spare rooms. It's cheap, it's honest, and it
asks nothing of you but effort. What it can't do is teach. It can't tell you your
hand dropped, that you're standing too square, that this is the round where it
counts. It just hangs there and takes what you give it.

A coach changes everything. A coach turns punching into boxing — gives the work
shape, corrects the small things before they become habits, and keeps you honest
in the round you'd rather coast. But a coach is expensive, or far away, or only
available at 6pm on Tuesdays. Most people who train never get one.

Corner exists to close that gap.

## Why most workout timers aren't enough

Open any interval-timer app and it does exactly one thing: it counts. Three
minutes, a beep, one minute, a beep. It measures your time and leaves the coaching
to you — which is fine if you already know what to do, and useless if you don't.

The beep is the tell. It's the sound of software that has nothing to say. It marks
the boundary and abandons you to the middle of the round, the part that actually
matters. A timer treats a workout as a stopwatch problem. Boxing is not a stopwatch
problem.

## Why coaching matters

The difference between a timer and a coach is judgement — knowing *what* to say,
*when* to say it, and, most of the time, choosing not to say anything at all.

A real coach works in waves. They set you off, let you find your rhythm, drop in a
cue, go quiet while you apply it, mark the time so you can pace yourself, push you
when it's time to push, and count you down at the end. They don't narrate. They
don't repeat the same line like a recording. They reinforce a lesson three
different ways until it sticks. Corner is built to do exactly that — not to talk
more, but to talk *better*.

## Why hands-free matters

You can't read a screen with gloves on and your eyes on the bag. The moment an app
asks you to look down, tap a control, or check a number, it has broken the thing
that makes training training.

So the voice is the product. You press Start, put the phone on the floor, and don't
touch it again until you're done. Everything else — the huge timer, the round dots,
the on-screen cue — is a caption for people who glance, not a control panel you have
to operate. If Corner is working, you forget the phone is there.

## Why trust matters

An athlete gives a coach their attention on the promise that it will be spent well.
Break that promise and the whole thing collapses.

That's why Corner's countdown is exact, every time — one mistimed "three, two, one"
and you stop believing anything the voice says. It's why the coach never claims to
see you ("your left is dropping" from an app that can't see is a lie you catch
instantly). It's why it never shames, never pushes you through pain, never breaks
character into app-speak. Trust is earned in the small, honest moments and spent in
an instant. The architecture — deterministic execution, exact timing, a coach that
only speaks when it has something worth your attention — exists to protect it.

## The long-term vision

Corner starts as a coach for the heavy bag. Where it goes:

- **Off the screen.** Wearables and audio-first devices, so the phone disappears
  entirely and it's just a voice in your corner.
- **Across your training.** Cloud sync so your coach remembers your work, your
  progress, and where you left off, on any device.
- **More voices.** A marketplace where real coaches author their own workouts and
  coaching personalities — their knowledge, in your corner, at any hour.
- **Smarter, carefully.** AI-assisted coaching that adapts to how you're actually
  moving — added only when it can be done without breaking the two things that
  matter most: determinism and trust.

The measure of success never changes: an athlete presses Start, puts the phone
down, trains hard, and finishes feeling coached by someone who cared — not narrated
at by software.

That's the whole idea. Everything else is engineering in service of it.
